# Microsoft Jigsaw—An Honest Critique

## Historical Context

Taking a beak from MMO gaming I decided I would download some casual games to play for relaxation. Seeing that in Windows 11 Microsoft had moved the card games to an app titled Microsoft Solitaire and Casual Games, I downloaded that and spent a few days playing old favorites and learning new solitaire card games. It wasn't long before I noticed the tiles advertising other downloadable casual games and seeing Microsoft Jigsaw listed sparked my hobbyist passion for physical jigsaw puzzles.

Past digital jigsaw puzzle games never really held my attention, so I thought I would give this a go and see if I liked it.  I quickly fell in love with the game; it had new takes with daily challenges including a Lock Breaker, Bug Catcher, and Jigsaw Jammer. These ideas were intriguing, and for many months I have continued to do the daily challenges as a quick unwind from a hard day's work. After doing those the first day I went back to the Classic Jigsaw to see how well that panned out.

## The Surprise

I was immediately surprised that of each of the pictures provided for free there were six dedicated sizes to chose from labeled thus:
Easy (24 pieces)
Medium (54 pieces)
Hard (94 pieces)
Expert (216 pieces)
Master (384 pieces)
Grandmaster (600 pieces)

While I have never considered myself a Grandmaster jigsaw puzzle builder, I have always known I have a special aptitude for them. A standard 500 piece real-world jigsaw puzzle for me is an afternoon or three- to four-hour project. The largest I have completed was 6,000 and that took me 11 years to complete. Still 1,000 and 2,000 are more my usual puzzle sizes; when I had desk space to build them.

Without dedicated desk space; I have wanted a digital facsimile to enjoy. Microsoft Jigsaw has been enjoyable, but 600 pieces is a valid user interface limit. A 600 piece puzzle in MS Jigsaw still takes me only about three to four hours to complete, most of that time is just organizing pieces instead of actively building.

## The Good

The first thing that stood out to me was the beveling and shadow effects on each piece giving them a real sense of depth. Just the right amount to appear as real puzzle pieces on a desk.

While the Jigsaw Jam and Jigsaw Jammer daily challenge feature non-traditional shapes, the Classic Jigsaw shapes are standard fully-interlocking pieces. These are the shapes I am accustom to and enjoy finding fits by looking for patterns along with colors.

The settings include nine desktop textured colors which can easily be switched to find a good contrast for just about any picture. Something not so easily changed on real-world desks.

Completing puzzles earns in-game currency which can be spent on new pictures and other puzzle configurations. In six months of casual play I earned about 100,000 “gold bars” plenty to keep me in new pictures indefinitely.

The desk space for each specific size of puzzle fits fairly well with the number of pieces.

The mouse scroll wheel zooms in making it easy to get a close detailed view of the image section on each piece and more nuance to the shape. While scrolling out can see basic shapes and colors for better overall picture or piece sorting.

## The Mediocre

That brings me to the not so nice, but not terrible, vertical and horizontal scroll. I have observed two methods to pan the desktop when zoomed in. The more natural while mousing is click-and-drag as if picking the entire desktop up and moving into a camera view. This adds to the click-strain on the finger (higher muscle tension) and is limited how far any direction necessitating multiple drag-and-drops.

The second way can be accomplished with the arrow keys on the keyboard. This would be my preference if it weren't for a clunky start and direction changes. The first press of an arrow key loads a marker or virtual mouse cursor.  This cursor can be moved about the screen and pressing space bar over a piece will pick that piece up and space bar again will drop it. If this virtual mouse cursor hits the sides of the zoomed in screen it will scroll; but to move the other direction the cursor must first move all the way across the visible desk space to another edge of the window to scroll in that direction. The scrolling action then also feels like it moves too fast.

It would not surprise me if this was original developed for touch screens and this virtual cursor was originally a tap or tap-and-drag mechanism. But on a desktop this is clunky.

If one desires, there is a lock view button that will override the click-and-drag desktop view. This allowed one to click and drag a selection over multiple pieces that can then be clicked and dragged to move all together. I find this helpful at the beginning of a large puzzle to quickly clear a square to start building edge pieces in.

A button to re-arrange pieces on the desktop exists; and randomly scatters them. An undo can send them right back to their prior position. This can help spread them out more but is not a very useful feature with large amounts of pieces.

While I miss the rotation of pieces so sorting by shape is easier; it is a good thing in this UI that all the pieces are oriented the direction they will fit. But I miss that challenge; and would prefer an app that does rotations.

## The Bad

Having three containers to sort pieces in should have been a good feature. But the implementation left so much to be desired that I have to count it as bad. First each piece must be meticulously clicked and dragged to a sorting box. Dropping the pieces from the main desktop to a mostly closed box tends to stack them right on the edge of the box. They can be moved around inside the box; but doing so means opening the box and dragging them individually around the confines of the box.

Pieces can be dragged from the desktop into an open box or out of an open box onto the desktop. But the box covers the majority of the desktop that this only works if the pieces are coming from or going to the edges of the desktop; or if zoomed one must scroll around to find the pieces or the positions to place them. The box, in true Microsoft fashion, can also be dragged around as a window within a window; but also not that helpful.

The entire box's contents can be emptied right onto the desktop, but they are emptied as is. If they are stacked on the edge they go to the desktop as one stacked pile. If they are spread out in the box they land the same way on the desktop, but in the center right over-top any other pieces existing there.

## The Ugly

Constant drag-and-drop for manipulating each piece gets tedious and causes muscle strain. Organizing pieces in any fashion requires repetitive drag-and-drop. For 600 piece puzzles my best strategy is to clear a space in the center, browse for all the edge pieces moving them to the center space. Then I build the edge, and position the rest of the pieces in a grid-like layout around the edge, moving them into the center as I sort colors and build small sections.

While this strategy works, there is no way I would want to do that on a 1,000 piece puzzle or greater. It is also the most time consuming aspect.

## Improvement Ideas

Even a simple snap-to-grid could speed up the dragging into a grid-like pattern for rapid piece scanning.

Better sorting features would be making sorting actually useful.

Hover and keystrokes would be better than constant mouse clicking.

Arrow key scrolling would be better than click-and-scroll or virtual mouse cursors.

And, on and on. There are so many ways I think this could be enhanced that I decided I might just build my own puzzle app.
